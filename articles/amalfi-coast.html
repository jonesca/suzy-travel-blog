Driving the Amalfi Coast: Terrifying, Beautiful, and Absolutely Worth It

Apr 10, 2026 · 7 min read · Amalfi Coast, Italy

The road along the Amalfi Coast is barely wide enough for two cars, the drop to the sea is two hundred metres straight down, and the drivers coming the other way treat the whole arrangement as a personal obstacle course. I was gripping the wheel so hard my knuckles whitened. I was also grinning. Both things were simultaneously true.

Why Drive It at All?

The sensible options are the ferry or the SITA bus. Ferries are beautiful, efficient, and let you look at the coast rather than sweating through it. Buses are cheap and run regularly and are driven by local men who navigate the hairpins with the casual authority of people who have done this exact thing ten thousand times and could do it asleep.

So why rent a car? Because the detours are where the coast lives. The road up to Ravello, fifteen minutes inland and a thousand metres higher, exists mostly outside the tourist bustle and rewards patience with the kind of views that make you understand why composers and writers came here and never quite left. And because driving slowly — really slowly, window down — through Positano at 7am, before it wakes up, is an experience no bus timetable can offer.

"The road is barely wide enough, the drop is vertiginous, and somewhere below is the most impossibly blue sea. Nothing about it should work. Everything about it does."

The Drive: Sorrento to Amalfi Town

Collect your car in Sorrento the evening before and drive to Positano early — ideally before 8am. The coast road, the SS163, officially begins where the town ends and immediately makes its intentions clear with a series of blind hairpins above a drop that will either thrill or horrify you depending on your relationship with heights.

Positano is where you make your first stop. Park at one of the hillside lots (expect to pay €5–8 for two hours) and walk down into the village through narrow staircases draped in bougainvillea. Eat a sfogliatella from the bakery on Via dei Mulini. Look at the beach from the terrace above it. The colours — white buildings, vivid pink flowers, impossibly blue water — are not retouched in the photographs. This is genuinely what it looks like.

Positano at 8am — all the beauty, a fraction of the crowds.

The Ravello Detour (Don't Skip It)

Most people stay on the coast road. Turn off at Atrani — the smallest municipality in Italy, worth thirty minutes just for its own sake — and follow the signs up to Ravello. The road climbs through lemon groves and olive terraces to a hilltop town that has been attracting serious-minded visitors since the Romans.

The gardens of Villa Cimbrone end at the Terrazza dell'Infinito — the Terrace of Infinity — a belvedere lined with marble busts that overhangs the coast 350 metres above the sea. It is one of those places that photography genuinely cannot capture. You stand there and feel the scale of it through your feet. Greta Garbo came here in 1938 and reportedly wept. I understand that impulse completely.

Where to Eat Along the Way

In Amalfi town: Trattoria da Gemma has been serving since 1872 and makes a scialatielli ai frutti di mare — local pasta with mixed seafood — that justifies the drive on its own. It fills up by noon; arrive early or book the evening before. In Positano, the honest answer is that most restaurants are tourist-priced and decent at best: the exception is Da Costantino, fifteen minutes up the hillside road, where you eat on a terrace with a view that renders the food almost irrelevant (though the pasta is excellent).

Stop at any roadside lemon stall you see. The Amalfi lemons — the sfusato amalfitano — are enormous, fragrant, and almost sweet. Buy a bottle of limoncello to take home. Buy two.

🚗 Practical Driving Notes

  • Drive west to east (Sorrento → Amalfi → Salerno): you stay on the cliff side, not the drop side
  • Avoid July and August entirely; the road becomes a car park
  • Start before 8am — tour buses don't run until 9am
  • The road is perfectly manageable in a small hatchback; don't hire anything larger
  • Parking in Positano: Parcheggio Internazionale, €5/hr, walk down from there

Pho for Breakfast: Eating My Way Through Hanoi's Old Quarter

Mar 14, 2026 · 6 min read · Hanoi, Vietnam

Broth that has been simmering since before sunrise, served in a bowl the size of your head on a plastic stool at the end of a lane barely wide enough for a bicycle. Hanoi mornings, I will argue with anyone, are the best mornings in the world.

The Ritual of the Morning Bowl

Pho in Hanoi is not a meal. It is a ritual, a greeting, a city-wide agreement to begin each day in the same deliberate way. By 6am the narrow streets of the Old Quarter are already loud with the sound of ladles hitting pots, vendors calling to each other across alleys, and the particular roar of a hundred motorbikes setting off on the first runs of the day.

The pho stalls operate on a schedule that respects no tourist's sleep pattern. They open at 5:30 or 6am and close when the pot is empty — often by 9 or 10am. This is the correct time to eat pho. If you sleep until noon and expect a bowl, you have misunderstood the assignment. Set your alarm. It is worth it.

"The broth has been simmering since 3am. You have been asleep. There is no competition between you and this soup — the soup wins."

The Anatomy of a Perfect Bowl

Hanoi pho — phở bò, the beef version — is a different creature from its southern cousin in Saigon. It is cleaner, less sweet, more restrained: a clear amber broth made from charred ginger and onion, star anise, cinnamon, and bones that have given up everything they had overnight. Into it goes flat rice noodle, sliced beef (rare, or well-done, or both — you choose), and a scattering of spring onion and coriander.

What goes on the table beside it: a plate of fresh herbs (basil, saw-tooth herb), lime wedges, sliced chilli, and quẩy — fried breadstick — for dipping. Do not add hoisin or sriracha. Not in Hanoi. This is considered approximately as subtle a breach of etiquette as putting ketchup on a fine wine.

Phở bò at Phở Gia Truyền on Bát Đàn — a masterclass in restraint.

Where to Go

  • Phở Gia Truyền — 49 Bát Đàn The most famous pho stall in Hanoi, open since the 1950s. Queue starts before 6am; arrive at 7am and you will wait twenty minutes in the street, which is part of the experience. Order the tái nạm (rare and brisket mixed) and do not rush.
  • Phở Thìn — 13 Lò Đúc The other school of Hanoi pho thought: the beef here is stir-fried in garlic before going into the bowl, giving the broth a richer, smokier character. Controversial among purists. Extraordinary in the bowl.
  • Phở Bà Đằng — 1 Hàng Muối Smaller, quieter, and harder to find — down an alley off the main street. The broth here is the most delicate of the three: complex and clear, with an almost floral quality from the star anise. Worth the extra effort of finding it.

Beyond Pho: The Rest of the Hanoi Morning

Pho is the beginning, not the whole. After your bowl — or instead of it, if you want to explore — the Old Quarter offers a full curriculum of morning food. Bánh mì filled with pâté and pickled daikon from carts that appear and vanish. Xôi xéo — sticky rice with mung bean and fried shallots — from the woman at the corner of Hàng Gai who has been there since dawn. Bún ốc, snail noodle soup, which sounds alarming and tastes extraordinary and which I ate for three consecutive mornings.

Budget about 50,000–80,000 VND (roughly €2–3) per bowl of pho. Eat on a plastic stool. Eat quickly — not because anyone will rush you, but because that is the pace of the morning and it feels right to match it. Then get back out into the city while the light is still low and the lanes are still manageable and Hanoi is still, briefly, itself.

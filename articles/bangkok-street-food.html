The Only Bangkok Street Food Guide You'll Ever Need

May 6, 2026 · 7 min read · Bangkok, Thailand

Pad see ew at midnight, mango sticky rice at dawn, and a bowl of boat noodles that permanently rearranged my understanding of what soup can be. Bangkok doesn't have a food scene — Bangkok is a food scene, wrapped loosely around some temples and traffic.

The First Rule of Bangkok Eating

Ignore restaurants with photographs of their dishes on the menu out front. This is not universal advice for all of Thailand, but in Bangkok it mostly holds. The best food is found at folding plastic tables on footpaths, at vendors who make exactly one thing — one thing — and have been making it for twenty years. A laminated menu with six hundred options is never going to beat a cart with a single wok and a woman who has been stirring pad kra pao since before you were born.

The second rule is to eat at the hours Thais eat: breakfast by 8am, lunch between 11 and 1pm, and then again at 9pm when the night markets wake up. The street food moves with the sun.

What to Eat and When

  • Jok (rice congee) — for breakfast, ideally from a cart near any market. Order it with a soft egg and sliced ginger. Cost: 40–60 baht.
  • Pad kra pao (basil stir-fry) — the true national dish, available everywhere, always served with a fried egg on top. Order it with pork and ask for phet (spicy). Cost: 60–80 baht.
  • Boat noodles — small, intensely dark bowls of broth thickened with pork blood and loaded with offal, herbs, and thin noodles. Order four. Cost: 15–25 baht per bowl at Khlong Lat Mayom floating market.
  • Som tum (green papaya salad) — pounded fresh to order, with dried shrimp, chili, fish sauce, and lime. The best version I ate was from a stall near Or Tor Kor market. Cost: 50 baht.
  • Khao niao mamuang (mango sticky rice) — for dessert, and yes, also for breakfast. The season peaks May–June; the mangoes are extraordinary. Cost: 60–100 baht.

Boat noodles at Khlong Lat Mayom — four bowls for less than the price of a coffee back home.

Where to Go

Yaowarat (Chinatown) is the essential Bangkok night food experience — go on a weekend after 6pm and walk slowly down the main road and the lanes branching off it. Stalls selling roast duck, char siu pork, fresh oyster omelettes, and enormous grilled prawns line every surface. It is loud, hot, and completely brilliant.

Or Tor Kor Market near Chatuchak is where Bangkok's chefs and serious cooks shop. The produce section alone is worth the trip — mountains of lychees, rambutan, young coconuts, and mangosteen arranged with what I can only describe as artisanal pride. Eat in the food hall upstairs: the crab fried rice is the best I've had anywhere.

Ari neighbourhood is where young Bangkokians eat and the scene has grown quietly excellent — less for street food specifically and more for the genre of small, experimental Thai places that have been appearing around the BTS stops. Walk from the station and follow your nose.

A Note on Spice

Thai chili heat operates on a different register to most cuisines I've eaten. The bird's eye chili — tiny, unprepossessing, devastating — is added freely and early. When ordering, you have options: mai phet (not spicy), phet nit noi (a little spicy), or phet mak (very spicy, do this if you want to have an experience). I recommend committing to phet nit noi for your first few meals until you've calibrated. You can always go hotter; you can't un-eat the bird's eye chili.

Carry cash in small notes. Most street vendors can't break a 500-baht bill. Budget 300–500 baht per day for street food and you will eat magnificently.

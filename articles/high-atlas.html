3 Days Trekking the High Atlas: A Beginner's Honest Account

Apr 22, 2026 · 9 min read · Morocco

I am not a hiker. I own one pair of proper boots purchased specifically for this trip, have the cardiovascular fitness of someone who considers a brisk walk to the café a workout, and have never slept in anything more rugged than an Airbnb with a broken radiator. This is the story of how I survived — and loved — three days in Morocco's most spectacular mountains.

Getting There: Imlil

The starting point for most High Atlas treks is Imlil, a small village about 60km south of Marrakech reached by a grand taxi that costs around 30 dirhams and winds through increasingly dramatic scenery for about ninety minutes. You will pass walnut orchards, terraced barley fields, and villages so old the walls seem to have grown from the rock.

I arrived in Imlil at 10am having booked nothing, which I don't recommend. A guide will approach you within three minutes of stepping out of the taxi. Mine was named Hassan, and he was both extremely patient and professionally amused by the state of my footwear.

⛰ Day One

Imlil to Refuge de Mouflon (4 hours)

The first day's walk follows the Aït Mizane valley, climbing gradually through terraced Berber villages where children run alongside the path and old men sit on walls watching you with benign curiosity. The trail is rocky in places but rarely technical. The altitude — we reached roughly 2,600m by camp — is the main challenge: the air thins quietly and the effort doubles without warning.

Hassan stopped me regularly. Not to rest, he insisted, but to look. The man has a gift for finding the exact moment the valley opens up below and the light is doing something extraordinary. I stopped arguing after the third time he was right.

The Aït Mizane valley opening out below — one of Hassan's strategic stops.

⛰ Day Two

The Summit Attempt (and the Lesson in Letting Go)

We set out at 5am for Jebel Toubkal, the highest peak in North Africa at 4,167m. The pre-dawn is astonishing — the stars at altitude are a different category of stars entirely, close enough to feel presumptuous. By 8am, however, I had reached a private conclusion: my body was done. My lungs were lodging a formal complaint and my legs had begun a quiet work-to-rule.

Hassan read the situation before I said anything. We found a flat rock at about 3,800m, sat on it, ate flatbread with olive oil, and looked at a view so enormous it became almost abstract. I did not reach the summit. The summit was extremely nice about it.

"The summit was at 4,167 metres. I stopped at 3,800. The view was indistinguishable. The flatbread was excellent."

⛰ Day Three

The Descent, the Hammam, the Tagine

Going down is harder on the knees than going up is on the lungs. This is trekking's most underrated piece of advice. Take poles if you have them. Walk slowly and diagonally on steep sections. Let your guide set the pace.

We were back in Imlil by 1pm. Hassan recommended a hammam in the village. I cannot stress enough how much a hammam means after three days of mountain camping. You will experience a level of physical restoration usually reserved for science fiction. Afterwards: a tagine of lamb with prunes and almonds in the guesthouse courtyard, a mint tea, and the deep, animal satisfaction of having put your body through something it wasn't sure about and come out the other side.

What I'd Tell a First-Timer

Hire a guide — not because the trail is dangerous but because it transforms the experience entirely. Hassan's knowledge of the plants, the villages, the routes, the people was a different journey than I'd have had alone. Expect to pay 400–600 dirhams per day for a certified mountain guide. Book accommodation in advance if you're going in April or August. Bring layers even in summer; the temperature drops 15°C between valley and summit. And lower your expectations of yourself: the mountain doesn't grade on ambition. It doesn't need to.

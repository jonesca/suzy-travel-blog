How I Became a (Temporary) Local in Lisbon for 30 Days

Mar 28, 2026 · 9 min read · Lisbon, Portugal

What started as a one-week trip to see a friend turned into a month of slow living in the neighbourhood I grew up calling home. Coming back to Lisbon not as a local and not quite as a tourist — as something in between — changed how I think about travel altogether.

Why Mouraria

I could have stayed in Chiado, which is beautiful and central and where most visitors end up. I chose Mouraria because my grandmother lived there, because it is the oldest neighbourhood in Lisbon and one of the least changed, and because it is where fado — the city's melancholic, salt-and-longing music — was born in the narrow lanes around the Castelo de São Jorge.

Mouraria is also increasingly fashionable, which I say not as a complaint but as a useful fact: it has the independent restaurants and small galleries that follow gentrification without yet having lost the old men playing cards outside the tabacaria or the women who lean from upstairs windows to call conversations down into the street below.

"There is a version of Lisbon that tourists see, and a version that reveals itself slowly — in the sound of a neighbour's radio through a thin wall, in the taste of coffee made exactly one way at exactly the right temperature."

The Daily Rhythm

I established a routine within the first week, which is the whole point of staying long enough. Every morning: coffee and a torrada — thick toast with butter, very Portuguese — at Café da Mouraria on Rua dos Lagares, where the owner knows my order by day four and never asks again. Then two hours of writing. Then errands: the Mercado de Campo de Ourique for vegetables, the talho on the corner for meat if I was cooking, or the tasca down the hill if I wasn't.

Afternoons were for walking — not the structured, sightseeing kind but the purposeless, get-lost kind that you can only do when you have nowhere to be. Lisbon rewards this more than almost any city I know. Its seven hills mean that every street ends in either a staircase or a view, often both.

The café on Rua dos Lagares where the morning always began.

What It Actually Costs

People ask about the money. Here is the honest version. I rented a one-bedroom apartment through a local agency — not Airbnb, which has contributed significantly to Lisbon's housing crisis and which I've stopped using — for €950 per month. The neighbourhood was far cheaper for food than the tourist areas.

What Slow Travel Actually Teaches You

A week in a city gives you its highlights. A month gives you its weather, its rhythms, its Tuesdays. I learned that Lisbon is a morning city — the light before 10am is extraordinary and the city is quiet enough to hear — and an evening city, when everyone moves to the miradouros to watch the sun set over the Tagus. The hours in between belong to the locals getting on with their lives.

I also learned that I had been travelling too fast. Two days here, three days there, the relentless optimisation of novelty. Thirty days of one place taught me more about Portugal than six previous visits combined. I understood the pace of a Sunday. I had a favourite fishmonger. My upstairs neighbour's name was Joaquim and he played guitar badly but with great feeling every evening at 7pm, and I came to love it.

That is what slow travel gives you: the small, unscheduled things that make a place feel briefly, beautifully yours.

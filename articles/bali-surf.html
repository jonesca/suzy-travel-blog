Learning to Surf in Bali at 29: Humbling, Hilarious, Addictive

Feb 10, 2026 · 8 min read · Bali, Indonesia

My instructor said I'd be standing by day two. He was being generous. By day two I had achieved a personal best of 1.4 seconds upright before the ocean made its opinion clear. This is an honest, unsentimental, and ultimately rather fond account of learning to surf at an age when your body files formal protests.

Why Canggu

Bali has more surf breaks than a month could cover. The famous ones — Uluwatu, Padang Padang, Keramas — require experience, respect, and some confidence in your own mortality. Canggu, on the island's southwest coast, has a different reputation: mellow beach breaks, a surf school on every corner, and the kind of warm, forgiving water that makes falling off feel almost intentional.

It is also, genuinely, one of the more enjoyable places to spend a week. Excellent coffee, good food at every price point, and an atmosphere that mixes surf culture with digital nomad café society in a way that shouldn't work but somehow does. I rented a room in a family guesthouse fifty metres from the beach for €22 a night and felt like I'd won something.

"The ocean does not care about your intentions. It will pick you up, spin you around, and deposit you face-first in the shallows with complete indifference. This is, eventually, the lesson."

The Diary

Day 1 — The Foam Board

My instructor was a quietly confident Balinese man named Wayan who had been surfing since he was five and teaching since he was sixteen. He gave me a foam board the size of a dining table and explained, with patient clarity, that I would begin by lying down. I felt this was reasonable and agreed. The lying-down part went well. Everything after that was more complicated.

Days 2–3 — The Pop-Up Problem

The pop-up — the movement from lying to standing in one fluid motion — is technically straightforward and physically counterintuitive in the way that most sports are. Wayan demonstrated it approximately forty times on the sand. I replicated it, on the sand, with something that was almost right. On the water: different story. I stood up twice on day two. For a total of perhaps three seconds of vertical time. I was unreasonably proud.

Day three — the foam board and I had reached an understanding. A tense one.

Days 4–5 — Something Shifted

On day four I stood up on a wave and rode it. Not far — maybe six metres — but the sensation of the board accelerating beneath me while I was, improbably, still on top of it was completely unlike anything I had anticipated. It felt like the ocean had briefly agreed to cooperate, and I understood why people organise their entire lives around chasing that feeling.

Days 6–7 — Wipeouts as Information

The final two days I surfed without Wayan — alone in the early morning, when the beach is quieter and the light is extraordinary. I fell more than I stood. I also learned that falling is not failure; it is the mechanism by which surfing teaches you everything. Each wipeout told me something about weight distribution, timing, body position. The ocean is a better teacher than any instructor, because it doesn't soften the feedback.

Would I Go Back?

Without hesitation. I returned to Lisbon with a new understanding of my own body, a deep respect for anyone who surfs competently, and a chronic desire to be in warm saltwater before breakfast. Canggu has its tourist-industrial edges — the smoothie bowls and "surf and yoga retreat" signs are relentless — but the ocean doesn't care about any of that. It's still there at 6am, doing exactly what it's always done, indifferent and magnificent and entirely worth the early alarm.

Budget about €30–40/day all-in for accommodation, lessons (€20 for two hours with board), and food. Stay at least a week. Start on the foam board and don't let ego rush you onto a shorter board. Eat nasi campur from the warungs near the beach for breakfast. Tip your instructor generously. Fall into the sea with good humour. That, mostly, is the whole of it.
